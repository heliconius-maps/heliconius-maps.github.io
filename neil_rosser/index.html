My research focuses on the origins and maintenance of biodiversity, using butterflies and their host plants as a study system. I am particularly interested in how geography, ecology and genetics interact to drive the evolution of new forms and species.

Research highlights

Hybrid speciation

Heliconius elevatus and H. pardalinus comprise a complex of incipient butterflies species distributed across the Amazon basin. Sympatric populations in western Amazonia have strong prezygotic isolation, mediated by ecological divergence and assortative mating (red and dark blue populations in map below). Nonetheless, they are completely interfertile. Genomic divergence between these populations is very low, and they are strongly differentiated across just ~2% of their genome (so-called "islands of divergence").

Quantitative trait loci mapping shows that multiple traits underpinning assortative mating (colour pattern, wing shape, pheromones, flight dynamics, host plant) map to the islands of divergence, which in turn appear introgressed from a third species, Heliconius melpomene. Taken together, these provide compelling evidence for hybrid speciation driven by adaptive intogression.

Rosser et al. 2019. Geographic contrasts between pre- and postzygotic barriers are consistent with reinforcement in Heliconius butterflies. Evolution 73(9):1821-1838.

Genetics of hybrid sterility

In contrast, allopatric populations of H. pardalinus (yellow and dark blue dots in the map above) mate freely when brought together in captivity, but female hybrids are sterile. To date, most research on the genetics of hybrid sterility has focused on model organisms with XY sex determination, in which males are heterogametic (XY) and females are homogametic (XX). However, in Lepidoptera (butterflies and moths) females are heterogametic (ZW) and males are homogametic (ZZ).

In Rosser et al. (2022), we analysed hybrid female sterility in H. pardalinus by QTL mapping. We found a large effect of the sex chromosome, due to a strong interaction between loci at either end of the sex chromosome. Somewhat counterintuitively, recombined sex chromosomes had higher fertility (see figure).

I am currently involved in a study led by Tianzhu Xiong, Harvard University, which applies similar methods to distantly related swallowtail butterflies. Suprisingly, we are finding very similar results – recombined sex chromosomes rescue hybrid fitness. This suggests that there may be generalities in the genetics of hybrid sterility in Lepidoptera.

Biogeography of the Amazon

The Amazon basin contains few obvious geographic barriers, yet it is the most biodiverse region on Earth. In heliconiine butterflies, maps made from museum specimens show that hybrid zones are clustered around the Amazon river, suggesting they promote divergence. However, maps of sampling effort also show a strong bias towards rivers, suggesting that the pattern might be an artifact.

Together with Prof. A. Freitas’ lab at the University of Campinas, I collected field samples along a 900km transect and showed that the Amazon river is indeed a biogeographic “suture zone” (an area where hybrid and contact zones of multiple taxa are clustered). This supports the riverine barrier hypothesis for high diversity in the Amazon, which dates back to Alfred Russell Wallace. We publicised the findings through an outreach film (below), which highlighted the critical importance of museums as invaluable resources of information on biodiversity.
